Introduction

The best deck: Kai’Sa

Results

Kai’Sa was able to take down the first Chinese regional to take place in Guangzhou, and also took 3rd and 4th place for an incredibly dominating performance. However, it was not the most popular deck in the top 64, despite making up 28% of the cut with 18 Kai’Sa decks advancing. Master Yi made up 42% of top 64 with 27 decks advancing, meaning Kai’Sa was able to consistently beat Master Yi in order to advance and take over top 4. Kai’Sa’s performance at the next regional, which took place in Beijing, was even more dominant. Of the 512 players in the event, 153 players brought a Kai’Sa deck, giving it the biggest chunk of the metashare at a staggering 30%. She had the highest count in top cut, with 34 Kai’Sa decks making top 64, giving her a staggering 53% meta share. This also gives her the highest top 64 conversion rate, sitting at 22%. Kai’Sa ended up taking 1st place again, and also made 4 of the top 8 spots once again for an incredibly dominating tournament performance. Similar results in almost every facet would occur at the Chongqing regional that followed, with Kai’Sa making up 33% of the field, and 24 of those decks making day 2 for a 14% conversion rate, which is a bit more reasonable. Again continuing the trend, Kai’Sa defeated Master Yi in the finals for yet another big win. Kai’Sa’s strength has persisted despite the metagame adapting to its power, with it continuing to represent in Chinese City Championships, taking down Hangzhou and Changsha. Finally, in the biggest tournament to date, the Chinese National Champions, Kai’Sa once again dominated, taking up 10 of the top 16 placements and winning the whole tournament, beating Master Yi in the finals.

Why is Kai’sa so strong?

Kai’Sa is very clearly a strong deck, arguably the strongest in the format. There isn’t exactly one particular reason why Kai’Sa is such a dominant archetype in the game. Instead, there are several strengths that all work together to make the deck such an oppressive force.

Legend Ability

The most straightforward answer for why Kai’Sa is so strong is the ability of her Legend card. It allows you to exhaust it and add 1 power of any color to spend exclusively on spells. While simple, this effect is incredibly strong, has zero drawbacks, and gives the deck an unmatched tool in winning the resource war. Effectively, over the course of several turns, it allows the Kai’Sa player to pull ahead of their opponent in the resource war by playing more power intensive spells without impacting their rune pool. This allows the deck to not only play more spells with power costs, but also to fire them off with impunity over the course of the game.

Falling Star


A key reason for why Kai’Sa is so strong is that Falling Star is the best card in the game, and Kai’Sa is the legend that is able to use it more effectively than any other legend, since her ability allows you to mitigate the otherwise high 2 power cost that casting it usually incurs. Falling Star is a card that is usually able to set your opponent’s board state back by an entire turn, either by removing a higher might unit or two smaller units. Its cheap Energy cost of 2 makes it incredibly cost efficient, as it is able to sometimes provide positive Energy swings of 3 or more by taking out chunkier units in one shot such as Master Yi, Honed, or an unbuffed Darius Trifarian, or by completely wiping out early game boards, like any combination of Stalwart Poro and Sunlit Guardian, an early board setup for most Calm decks. It also allows Kai’Sa players to remove them without having to interact with their Shield abilities, and the 2 power cost of Falling Star prevents Defy from being used to counter it, forcing players to use the much more expensive and much less common Wind Wall if they want to interact with the spell. The biggest part of Falling Star that makes it so powerful is its ability to target units in your opponent’s base, which very few other cards are able to do, especially at the cheap Energy cost of 2. This allows Kai’Sa to shut down attempts by opponents to develop units in their base.

Draw Engine

Kai’Sa has arguably the best draw engine in the entire game. Kai’Sa, Survivor is one of the most consistent sources of draw in the game and, if you go second She also has strong synergy with the spell Retreat, which is essentially a free to cast spell due to it channeling an exhausted rune when you cast it, and it allows you to replay your Kai’Sa, Survivor the following turn, which will most likely enter ready due to her Accelerate ability. In her colors of Mind and Chaos, she also has access to removal cards that provide draw, such as Void Seeker and Stupefy, as well as cheap units that provide card draw like Watchful Sentry and Lecturing Yordle. There is even the option to run Consult the Past, which most Kai’Sa decks choose not to run because the deck already has more than enough draw power in it.

Efficient Removal

Kai’Sa decks have access to a slew of excellent cheap removal cards and combat tricks that allow it to deal with almost any threat that comes its way. It has access to Stupefy and Smoke Screen, which are both cheap combat tricks that can turn the tide of almost any fight, especially combined with the removal spells the deck has access to, such as Hextech Ray and Void Seeker. The combination of Smoke Screen and Void Seeker can remove units with up to 8 might, which is quite a lot for only having to expend 5 Energy and 2 Power. Kai’Sa also has access to her signature spell, Icathian Rain, which allows you to spread 12 damage in chunks of 2 anywhere you want. This is incredible in almost any situation and is only really punished by big units with Deflect (looking at you, Deadbloom Predator) and is especially powerful against decks that want to swarm smaller units, such as Viktor and Teemo. Some other removal options Kai’Sa has access to are Singularity, Riptide Rex, Thermo Beam, and Iron Ballista. Singularity provides the deck with a powered up Falling Star that can be useful in certain matchups, and Riptide Rex provides a burst of removal that is attached to a unit which allows for lines that create additional tempo. Thermo Beam provides a quick response to decks that utilize Gear, which is critical against decks that are built around Dazzling Aurora, which is primarily featured in Miss Fortune and some Master Yi builds. Iron Ballista provides solid, consistent removal for smaller units and is very useful in specific matchups. Thermo Beam and Iron Ballista are cards that are more at home in the sideboard than in the main deck, but are nonetheless worthwhile considerations.

Battlefield Synergies

Kai’Sa has three battlefields that she has excellent synergy with.

  1. Void Gate
    The first and most obvious of these is Void Gate, which causes all spells and abilities that affect units there to do 1 extra damage. This is quite powerful for a deck like Kai’Sa, especially against decks that do not run many cards that can take advantage of noncombat damage, such as Master Yi. Getting the extra 1 damage on spells can also be the difference against decks that play combat tricks that buff their own units, such as Discipline, En Garde, and Cleave.
  2. Targon’s Peak
    Another battlefield that Kai’Sa loves to have in play is Targon’s Peak. Being able to ready 2 runes going into your opponent’s turn is incredibly powerful, especially for a deck that is so proficient at conquering early and often with Kai’Sa, Survivor, and has access to such strong and cheap interaction. If you go second and on your second turn you play an accelerated Kai’Sa, Survivor and conquer Targon’s Peak, those two runes could be enough to cast a Smoke Screen, or a combination of Hextech Ray and Stupefy, more than enough to beat almost anything any deck can respond with on turn 3.
  3. Reaver’s Row
    The third battlefield that Kai’Sa has is Reaver’s Row, and the synergy is specifically with two units that are commonly included in her deck: Kai’Sa, Survivor and Darius, Trifarian. Reaver’s Row allows for the preservation of these units in combat, both of which are incredibly valuable. Preserving Kai’Sa, Survivor allows for her conquer effect to be activated again the following turn, providing more resource velocity, and Darius, Trifarian allows for big tempo pushes to be created the following turn, as his ability allows him to get up to 7 might, potentially 10 might with Cleave, which is enough to win most combats.

Matchups

Ahri/Master Yi

Ahri and Master Yi end up being very similar matchups, as your primary goal is to play around their respective Legend abilities and the Shield units they both tend to run, both of which punish you for attacking into battlefields that they control. The solution to this problem is heavy removal and interaction. By using spells outside of combat, we are able to circumvent their strength at holding in combat and are able to establish control of the pace of the game. From there, it is usually not too difficult to keep the point lead or just prevent them from ever taking control of a battlefield again, severely limiting their ability to generate tempo. Falling Star is incredibly impactful in these matchups, as it is usually able to remove multiple early threats that these decks like to try and develop, such as Stalwart Poro and Sunlit Guardian.

Miss Fortune/Master Yi with Dazzling Aurora


These two decks are also very similar matchups, as both have the same gameplan of ramping into a fast Dazzling Aurora and then attempting to win the game through the value engine of playing high might, defensive units to lock the opponent out of retaking a battlefield. This is a matchup where sideboarding is very important, as a standard main deck for Kai’Sa will simply not have the tools to beat this matchup. The key cards for winning this matchup are Thermo Beam, Unchecked Power, and Dr. Mundo, Expert. Thermo Beam is the best response to destroying Dazzling Aurora as soon as possible to limit the value your opponent is able to get out of it. Unchecked Power allows you to clear out any board state if the Aurora player is ever able to establish control of both battlefields, and Dr. Mundo is the only unit Mind decks have access to that is ever able to stand up to the massive Deadbloom Predators and Mindsplitters that Aurora decks are typically packing. The inclusions of these cards in a useful quantity in your sideboard should be enough to swing the matchup from unfavorable to favored.

Teemo

Teemo is an unusual matchup due to all of the hidden cards that the deck packs, giving them a way to establish resources in a way that you cannot directly affect. However, you can affect them indirectly: by removing any unit they have at battlefields to force them to either reveal their hidden cards or send them to the trash without any return on their investment. Teemo decks also tend to run out of steam in the late game, and in the testing I have done for this matchup, you are typically able to outmuscle Teemo decks in the late game and control both battlefields, preventing them from playing hidden cards and shutting down the main strength of their deck.

Darius

Darius is an interesting matchup because both Kai’Sa and Darius tend to include a lot of the same cards in their deck. Both use Darius, Trifarian and Kai’Sa, Survivor as their primary units, both include Falling Star and in a few cases Void Seeker as their removal spells of choice, and both decks typically include 2 to 3 copies of Cleave in their main deck. Darius tends to be a more unit focused deck that pushes lots of units cheaply, with Noxus Hopeful being the best example of this. This matchup tends to devolve into a grindy slog, with the Darius player trying to outrace the Kai’Sa removal. Thousand-Tailed Watcher is a very high impact card in this matchup, as Darius cannot usually do much to defend against a push on the turn it hits the board and its ability is activated. The most difficult part about dealing with Darius is Vayne, who can hit the board turn after turn and pressure down your units while being rather difficult to remove due to her ability that allows her to be returned to hand when she conquers. Despite that, so far the matchup does seem to slightly favor Kai’Sa as Darius decks tend to run out of steam and Kai’Sa is able to draw more cards and maintain tempo.

Sett

This is the matchup that I have tested the least. The matchup seems to be a skewed towards unfavorable unless you have Time Warp in your deck, which flips the matchup a bit. Other than that, well timed removal and interaction spells are your path to victory, as well as including bigger units in your deck to help you scrap with Sett and his higher Might units. Dr. Mundo and Mega-Mech fulfill this role quite well, allowing you to score critical holds in the late game or to simply trade 1 for 1 with the buffed units Sett decks love to throw at you.

Mirror

If the trends we have seen at the early Chinese Riftbound events continue in North America of the tournament field being composed of very high counts of Kai’Sa decks, choosing to bring Kai’Sa to a big event is essentially a choice to encounter the mirror at least once at a big event, making it an eventuality that requires preparation. The single best card you can include for this matchup is a full three copies of Retreat. This allows you to protect your units from your opponent’s removal spells and save them from combats that they would otherwise die in from the opposing Kai’Sa’s combat tricks. The other big inclusion that eases the difficulty of the matchup is including bigger units in either your main deck or side board, such as Dr. Mundo, Draven, and Thousand-Tailed Watcher. Another potential inclusion that has shown promise in this matchup is Immortal Phoenix, whose ability allows you to consistently return it to the field making it a fairly removal proof threat, which is invaluable in this matchup.

Conclusion

Kai’Sa has proven itself in the Chinese Riftbound meta to be one of if not the most dominant archetypes in the entire game, and I expect this trend to continue as the metagame continues to develop in North America.
